## 1. Prerequisites & Language Choice

<details>
<summary><b>Choosing Your Programming Language</b></summary>

### Pick the language you know best

Interviewers evaluate **your problem-solving skills**, not your favorite programming language.

Your language should help you think clearly and implement solutions quickly—not slow you down because you're fighting syntax.

If you're significantly more comfortable with Java, C++, Go, or JavaScript, use that language.

### If you have multiple options, choose Python

If you're equally comfortable with several languages, **I recommend using Python**.

Why?

* Less verbose syntax.
* Faster to write under time pressure.
* Easier to debug.
* Built-in data structures (`dict`, `set`, `deque`, `heapq`) make many interview problems simpler.

Remember: interviewers care far more about your algorithm than how many lines of code you wrote.

### Don't switch languages before interviews

A common mistake is learning a new language a few weeks before interviews because someone said it's "better."

Don't.

Interview stress is already high enough. You don't want to waste mental energy remembering syntax or library functions.

Master one language and stick with it throughout your preparation.

</details>

<details>
<summary><b>Check the Company's Interview Rules</b></summary>

Before every interview, verify:

* Which programming languages are supported.
* Which platform will be used (CodeSignal, HackerRank, Codility, custom editor, etc.).
* Whether you can use your own IDE or must code in the browser.
* Whether autocomplete is available.
* Whether you can run your code before submitting.

These small details can make a big difference on interview day.

Never assume every company follows the same format.

</details>

<details>
<summary><b>Online Assessment (OA)</b></summary>

For many companies, the OA is the first technical screening.

Depending on the company, you may receive:

* 2–4 coding problems.
* Around 60–90 minutes.
* Sometimes additional work-style or behavioral questions.

The questions are usually LeetCode-style and often increase in difficulty.

### Don't expect to finish everything

Many candidates believe they need a perfect score.

You usually don't.

The OA exists primarily to reduce thousands of applicants to a smaller interview pool.

It's common for the final question to be considerably harder than the others.

Focus on maximizing your score instead of obsessing over solving every single problem perfectly.

### Time management matters

A common mistake is spending 40 minutes trying to optimize one difficult question.

Instead:

* Solve the easier questions first.
* Pass as many test cases as possible.
* Skip a problem temporarily if you're completely stuck.
* Return later if time allows.

A strong partial submission is often better than leaving multiple questions blank.

</details>

<details>
<summary><b>Behavioral Interviews</b></summary>

Behavioral interviews are often underestimated.

Many companies either:

* schedule a dedicated behavioral interview, or
* spend the first 10–15 minutes of every technical interview asking behavioral questions.

Prepare stories about:

* teamwork
* leadership
* conflict resolution
* failure
* difficult decisions
* learning something quickly
* challenging projects

Use the **STAR** framework, but don't memorize scripts.

The goal is to sound natural, reflective, and authentic.

</details>

<details>
<summary><b>Technical Interviews</b></summary>

This is where you'll solve coding problems while communicating with an interviewer.

Interviewers aren't just evaluating whether you find the optimal solution.

They're also observing whether you can:

* understand the problem
* ask clarifying questions
* explain your reasoning
* identify trade-offs
* write clean code
* analyze time and space complexity
* test your own solution
* respond well to hints

Communication is part of the evaluation.

Think out loud.

Explain what you're trying.

Say why you're choosing a particular data structure.

Even if you make mistakes, interviewers want to see how you recover.

</details>

<details>
<summary><b>Final Advice Before You Start Studying</b></summary>

Don't optimize your preparation for passing Online Assessments.

Optimize it for becoming genuinely good at solving unseen problems.

Companies change interview platforms.

Question banks evolve.

Difficulty varies every year.

Strong fundamentals in data structures, algorithms, communication, and problem-solving remain valuable regardless of the company or interview format.

That's what ultimately leads to offers.

</details>



### 2. Data Structures & Algorithms (DSA) Foundations
<details>
<summary><b>Click to expand: Core Topics to Master Before Grinding</b></summary>

Do not jump straight into LeetCode without knowing the theoretical foundations and implementation details of:

* **Arrays & Hashing:** Time/Space complexity of lookups, insertions, and deletions.
* **Two Pointers & Sliding Window:** Optimizing $O(N^2)$ brute-force solutions to $O(N)$.
* **Linked Lists:** Pointer manipulation, reversing, and cycle detection.
* **Trees & Graphs:** BFS, DFS, Recursion, and Tree traversals (In-order, Pre-order, Post-order).
* **Heaps / Priority Queues:** Top-K elements problems.
* **Dynamic Programming:** Start with the basics (Memoization vs. Tabulation).
</details>

### 4. Essential Communication & Behavioral Cheat Sheet
<details>
<summary><b>Click to expand: Interview Day Mechanics (Do Not Skip)</b></summary>

Technical perfection is useless if you cannot communicate. 

* **Think Out Loud:** Never stay silent for more than 30 seconds. Explain your thought process, trade-offs, and brute-force approach before writing code.
* **Clarify the Problem:** Ask about edge cases (e.g., empty inputs, negative numbers, size constraints) before proposing a solution.
* **Behavioral (STAR Method):** Prepare 3-4 solid projects/stories structured around:
  * **S**ituation: What was the context?
  * **T**ask: What was the challenge?
  * **A**ction: What did *you* do? (Focus on your contribution, not just the team's).
  * **R**esult: What was the quantifiable outcome?
</details>
